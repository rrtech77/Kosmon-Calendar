Introduction

Oahspe set out the principles of the Kosmon calendar in the Book of Inspiration. Because there were differing views about these principles, it became necessary to arrive at a resolution so that people of the world could be in accord with heaven when observing the holy days and Sabbaths.

These principles are centred on the orbit of the sun as the measure of the year, as opposed to counting the earth's rotations from an arbitrary historical event, or from a moon phase. Oahspe marks the beginning of this yearly orbit by the northern line of the sun — the Tropic of Cancer — the latitude at which the sun stands directly overhead at its northernmost point. The moment the sun reaches this line each year is the June solstice. It is this astronomical event, not any humanly devised calendar convention, that anchors the Kosmon year.

When naming the days of the year, Oahspe counts from the day of the solstice: Day 1 is the first day of the year, Day 7 is the first Sabbath, and so on. If Day 1 begins at the solstice moment, a question naturally follows: where on the globe does the new day — and therefore the new year — first begin?

In the civil calendar, the date changes at midnight at the International Date Line, a fixed boundary near the 180th meridian. But if the Kosmon year is to begin precisely at the solstice, and a day begins at midnight, then the natural starting boundary for Day 1 is the longitude where midnight coincides with the solstice moment — what this calendar calls the solstice dateline or midnight longitude point. Because the solstice moment advances roughly a quarter of a day each year through the leap-year cycle, this dateline shifts approximately 90° westward each year, cycling through all longitudes over four years. It will only rarely align near the International Date Line.

A consequence of anchoring the day count to this midnight longitude is that the Kosmon New Year's Day — and every Sabbath thereafter — falls on the same calendar day for all people around the world, regardless of their location. Holy days observed by any community using the solstice dateline are thus shared across all nations on one and the same day — a matter of particular significance for those who observe days of worship according to a precisely designated day of the week. How this shared day progresses around the globe with the turning of the Earth is demonstrated in the final section of this document.

A Wider Benefit: Global Media, Commerce and World Affairs

The advantages of a universally shared calendar day are not limited to those with a religious interest. Any system in which a given day is genuinely the same day everywhere on Earth offers immediate practical benefits to a wide range of global activities.

For international news media and broadcasting organisations, a shared day simplifies the dating of events considerably. At present, a major event occurring late on one side of the International Date Line falls on a different calendar date to observers on the other side — creating ambiguity in headlines, archives, and broadcasts. A solstice-anchored dateline, producing a day shared simultaneously across all time zones, would eliminate this class of confusion entirely. A news event, a market movement, a summit declaration — all would carry a single unambiguous date, understood identically in Tokyo, London, New York, and Sydney.

For world trade and financial markets, the benefits are similarly concrete. International contracts, settlement dates, delivery schedules, and regulatory filings all depend on an agreed calendar date. Where that date is currently subject to the quirk of which side of the Date Line a transaction falls on, a universally shared day removes the ambiguity. Globally coordinated market hours, cross-border legal instruments, and multinational corporate reporting would all gain clarity.

Broader still, the coordination of international scientific programmes, satellite operations, global health monitoring, environmental reporting, and intergovernmental agreements all involve the precise dating of events across jurisdictions. Any framework that places all nations unambiguously on the same calendar page — without the current patchwork of date-line exceptions — offers a small but genuine improvement in the coherence of global coordination.

None of these benefits require the world to abandon the Gregorian calendar for everyday civil use. They arise simply from the principle — already demonstrated by the International Date Line in its current form — that a dateline anchored to a natural astronomical event can serve as a shared reference point for all peoples. The Kosmon calendar proposes that such a reference point be the June solstice: the most ancient, universal, and astronomically precise marker available.

This transformation is not envisioned as a decree imposed by any world authority, but as a natural and gradual development as people come to recognise its value for religious observance — and only if the world of commerce were to follow in its own time could such a change become truly universal.

Detailed Explanation of the Sacred Kosmon Calendar

The Year

A year is the orbital period of the Earth around the Sun — the time it takes the Sun to appear to complete one full circuit through the sky and return to the same position relative to the stars and the seasons. The reference point for the Kosmon calendar is the beginning of the seventh era of Kosmon.

The precise length of the tropical year — the time from one June solstice to the next — is approximately 365 days, 5 hours, 48 minutes, and 45 seconds. This is not a whole number of days, and no calendar that tracks the solar cycle can avoid this inconvenience. The Gregorian calendar manages it by adding a leap day every four years (with century-year corrections), so that the average year length of 365.2425 days stays close to the true tropical year of 365.24219 days — an accuracy of about one day per 3,300 years.

The Kosmon calendar takes a different approach. Rather than inserting leap days to reconcile the fractional remainder, it counts 365 whole days from solstice to solstice as the practical length of the year. The remaining fraction — approximately 5 hours and 49 minutes — is not incorporated into the day count. Instead, the solstice dateline simply shifts to the new midnight longitude each year, automatically reflecting the true moment of the solstice. This means the Kosmon year always begins at the actual astronomical event, not at a conventional approximation of it.

Monthly divisions follow the lunar cycle, each month beginning at the new moon. Because the lunar month averages approximately 29.5 days and the solar year contains approximately 12.37 lunar months, a Kosmon year will have either 12 or 13 months. The monthly cycle is allowed to overlap the new year's day and recommences at the first new moon of the new year. This approach, combining a solar year with lunar months without forcing them into arithmetic conformity, produces a calendar that tracks both the seasons and the moon with natural accuracy — and provides a reliable framework for the tables of prophecy described in Oahspe.

A Brief History of Calendar Reform

Prior to Julius Caesar's reform of 46 BC, the Roman calendar had drifted far from alignment with the seasons through a combination of political manipulation and accumulated error. The Julian calendar, introduced in 45 BC, established the 365-day year with a leap day every four years — a significant improvement. However, the Julian year of 365.25 days is slightly longer than the true tropical year, and over the centuries this small error accumulated. By the sixteenth century, the accumulated drift amounted to about ten days, causing the spring equinox — which the Christian church needed to fall near 21 March for the calculation of Easter — to occur instead around 11 March.

The Gregorian calendar, introduced in 1582, corrected this by dropping ten days from October of that year and refining the leap year rule: century years are not leap years unless divisible by 400. This gives an average year length of 365.2425 days, much closer to the tropical year. The last day of the Julian calendar was Thursday, 4 October 1582, followed immediately by Friday, 15 October 1582 in the new system. Britain and its colonies adopted the Gregorian calendar in 1752; by 1929 it had been adopted by all major nations.

These reforms were driven by a need to keep Easter correctly positioned relative to the spring equinox — a concern of the Church, not of astronomical precision for its own sake. The Kosmon calendar is not subject to this constraint. Its year begins at the June solstice, an astronomically fixed moment determined by observation each year, making drift impossible by design.

The Tropical Year and Precession

The tropical year — the year measured from solstice to solstice — has a current mean value of approximately 365.2421897 days (365 days, 5 hours, 48 minutes, 45.2 seconds). This value changes very slowly over long periods of time due to gravitational interactions with other planets.

It is worth noting that the Gregorian calendar was designed to track the vernal equinox year specifically, and does so with high accuracy. However, the equinox itself precesses — it moves slowly backwards through the constellations of the zodiac at a rate of about 50 arc seconds per year, completing a full circuit in approximately 25,800 years. This precession is a feature of the Earth's axial wobble, and it does not affect the accuracy of either the Gregorian or the Kosmon calendar as timekeepers of the seasons. Both remain aligned with the actual astronomical events they are designed to track.

Solar Time and the Equation of Time

Solar time is time measured by the position of the sun in the sky. Apparent solar time is what a sundial reads — the actual position of the sun. Mean solar time is the uniform average, as shown by a well-regulated clock. The difference between the two is called the equation of time, and it arises from two causes.

First, the Earth's orbit is an ellipse. The Earth moves faster when closer to the sun (perihelion, around 3 January) and slower when farther away (aphelion, around 4 July). This means the sun appears to move across the sky faster at some times of year than others, contributing a variation with an amplitude of about 7.7 minutes and a period of one year.

Second, the Earth's axis is tilted 23.5° relative to its orbital plane. Because the sun moves along the ecliptic — a path tilted relative to the celestial equator — its daily eastward motion does not translate uniformly into a change of right ascension (the coordinate along which clock time is measured). Near the equinoxes, more of the sun's motion is in declination (north-south) and less in right ascension, effectively slowing the sun's apparent clock-time motion. Near the solstices the reverse is true. This obliquity effect contributes a variation with an amplitude of about 9.9 minutes and a period of half a year.

Together these two effects produce the equation of time — an irregular curve ranging from about +16 minutes 33 seconds (around 3 November, when the sundial runs fast) to about −14 minutes 6 seconds (around 12 February, when the sundial runs slow).

For the purposes of the Kosmon calendar, the equation of time is relevant to understanding why mean solar time — rather than apparent solar time — is used to locate the solstice dateline. The solstice moment is defined in terms of the sun's astronomical position (the moment it reaches its maximum declination), which is computed in Terrestrial Time and can be converted to Universal Time (UT) for practical use. The equation of time does not affect this calculation, but it does explain why local noon and clock noon differ, and why the midnight longitude point must be calculated from UT rather than from a sundial reading.

Sidereal Time

A sidereal day is the time it takes the Earth to complete one rotation relative to the distant stars: approximately 23 hours, 56 minutes, and 4.091 seconds. A solar day is approximately 4 minutes longer because during the time it takes the Earth to rotate once, it has also moved a short distance along its orbit, so the sun appears to have shifted slightly eastward and the Earth must rotate a little further to bring the sun back to its highest point.

Over the course of a year, this difference accumulates to one full day: there are 366.25 sidereal days in a solar year but only 365.25 solar days. Sidereal time is used by astronomers to locate stars and to define the orientation of the Earth's rotation axis, but for the purposes of the Kosmon calendar, mean solar time — and Universal Time derived from it — is the relevant standard.

Standard Time and Universal Time

Before the introduction of standard time zones in the late nineteenth century, every town set its clocks by local solar noon. This became impractical with the coming of railways and the telegraph, which required coordinated schedules across large distances. Sandford Fleming proposed a system of 24 standard time zones in 1879, each spanning 15° of longitude and differing from its neighbours by exactly one hour. The 1884 International Meridian Conference adopted the Greenwich meridian as the prime meridian and Greenwich Mean Time (GMT) as the world's time reference, because by that date two-thirds of all nautical charts already used it.

In 1928, Universal Time (UT) was adopted as a more precise replacement for GMT, defined by the rotation of the Earth and kept in step with atomic clocks through the occasional addition of leap seconds (Coordinated Universal Time, UTC). For practical purposes, UT and UTC differ by less than one second, and both serve as the reference for the Kosmon solstice calculations given in this document.

The key point for the Kosmon calendar is this: the solstice moment is an astronomical event that occurs at a specific instant in Universal Time. The longitude where midnight (00:00 local mean time) coincides with that instant becomes the Kosmon dateline for that year — the boundary from which Day 1 of the new year is reckoned.

The Dateline and Universal Time

Meridians and the International Date Line

A meridian is an imaginary line running from the North Pole to the South Pole along a fixed longitude. The Prime Meridian is defined as 0° and passes through the Royal Observatory at Greenwich, London — a choice made at the 1884 International Meridian Conference for reasons of navigational convention, not astronomical significance. The International Date Line runs approximately along the 180th meridian, on the opposite side of the globe from Greenwich, with diversions to avoid dividing island groups and territories.

Together, the Prime Meridian and the International Date Line divide the Earth into Eastern and Western Hemispheres. The date changes from one day to the next as the midnight line sweeps westward around the globe. At any given moment, the International Date Line marks the boundary between the two calendar dates currently in effect on Earth.

Both the Prime Meridian and the International Date Line are arbitrary human conventions. No international treaty fixes the exact position of the Date Line; all nations define their own time zones. It is placed in the Pacific Ocean for practical reasons — to minimise the inconvenience of a date change crossing inhabited land. As the example of Kiribati illustrates, when a dateline does bisect a nation's territory, it creates real difficulties: upon independence in 1979, the new republic found government offices on opposite sides of the line able to communicate only on the four weekdays when both sides simultaneously experienced working days. Kiribati resolved this in 1995 by moving the date line eastward to encompass all of its territory.

The date changes at midnight wherever you stand — the dateline simply marks the agreed boundary at which the count of the calendar date officially resets. Moving that boundary to the solstice midnight longitude does not alter the experience of any individual day; it only relocates the point on the globe where the new year's count begins.

Confusion about which day an event falls in — when comparing locations in different countries — arises not from the dateline itself, but from the difference in local time between one time zone and another. This comparison works identically whether or not the dateline is at Greenwich's antipode or at the solstice midnight longitude.

The Prime Meridian is likewise an arbitrary convention, set at Greenwich by historical precedent. For the Kosmon calendar, a floating dateline — reset each year to the solstice midnight longitude — serves the religious purpose of ensuring that New Year's Day and all Sabbaths are celebrated on the same calendar day around the world. Since the solstice midnight longitude advances approximately 90° westward each year through the four-year cycle (as the fractional-day remainder accumulates), it will pass through different continents in different years, but this is of no concern to those observing the holy days. The day count always starts afresh from the true solstice moment.

A further observation, from another community member:

"The only thing a time zone does is to more or less average out when exactly the midnight hour is for that geographical area within the standard time zone."

This is correct. Standard time zones are averages; only at the central meridian of a zone does clock midnight correspond exactly to solar midnight. The Kosmon dateline, by contrast, is defined by true solar midnight at the solstice moment — the most astronomically precise reference available.

The dateline is reset each year to the longitude where midnight falls at the moment of the solstice. The position of existing civil time zones is not affected. What changes is only this: the day on which the Kosmon New Year begins — and by extension all Sabbaths of that year — is the same calendar day for all peoples around the world.

Calculating the Solstice Dateline

The moon phase times given in the Kosmon Calendar tables below are in UKT (Universal Kosmon Time). Moon phase times are normally published in Greenwich time (UT), but to position them correctly within the Kosmon calendar, they must be converted from UT to UKT. This is done by subtracting the time difference between the new dateline longitude and Greenwich.

The conversion formula is straightforward: the solstice occurs at a known UT time. The solstice dateline is the meridian where local midnight coincides with that UT time. If the solstice occurs at time T (UT), then the dateline longitude is:

Dateline longitude = (T in hours × 15°) West of Greenwich

For the 2026 solstice, occurring at 08:24 UT, the dateline longitude is 8.4 hours × 15° = 126° West of Greenwich — solar midnight near Eureka, California.

To convert a moon phase time from UT to UKT, subtract the solstice time (in hours) from the phase time.

How Old Year's Day Travels the Globe

One might expect that a day shared by all peoples everywhere would require everyone to observe it at the same instant — a fixed universal moment, like a world clock striking simultaneously. The Kosmon calendar works quite differently, and the tables below show why this matters and how the shared day actually unfolds.

Because the Earth rotates, midnight does not arrive everywhere at once. It sweeps westward around the globe, reaching each location in turn. When Old Year's Day first begins at the Kosmon dateline at midnight, cities to the east have already entered that calendar day (their midnight fell earlier), while cities to the west are still in the day before (their midnight comes later). Over the course of the day, midnight reaches every remaining location in sequence — and with it, Old Year's Day begins for each place in turn. This is precisely the meaning of the phrase "even with the travel of the sun."

The example below uses the 2026 solstice, when the Kosmon dateline falls at approximately 126° West — solar midnight near Eureka, California, at 08:24 UT on 21 June 2026. The tables show three snapshots as that midnight sweeps around the world.

The Principle: Earth's Rotation Carries Midnight Westward

The Earth rotates anticlockwise when viewed from above the North Pole, which is why the sun appears to rise in the east. Midnight sweeps westward: cities to the east of the dateline entered June 21 first; cities to the west enter it last. The dateline at 126° West marks the longitude from which the Kosmon year-count begins — it is the last point on Earth to see the sun reach the Tropic of Cancer, and the first point to open the new day-count at its midnight. Honolulu, being the furthest west of the major cities shown, is the last to enter June 21.

The Shared Day — and What Oahspe Means

"And it shall come to pass that the Sabbath days all around the world shall be the same day unto all people, even with the travel of the sun." The Book of Inspiration, Ch. XIV, Verse 27

The tables confirm two things. First, June 21st is genuinely Old Year's Day for all peoples around the world — whether in Sydney, London, Mumbai, or Los Angeles, the date is the same. Second, each place enters that day at its own local midnight, as the Earth's rotation carries midnight progressively westward around the globe. This is what "even with the travel of the sun" means: not a fixed simultaneous moment imposed from outside, but the same day arriving naturally for each people as the Earth turns — each community free to celebrate at a time of their own choosing on that shared day, whether dawn, High Noon, sunset, or evening.

It is also worth noting how swiftly the circuit completes. For the 2026 solstice, from the moment Eureka strikes midnight to the moment Honolulu — the last major city in the westward sweep — enters Old Year's Day is just 1 hour and 36 minutes. Within less than two hours of the solstice, the entire inhabited world shares the same holy day. The celebration then unfolds through its full 24 hours as each community, on whatever continent, takes up the day in its own time and in its own way.
