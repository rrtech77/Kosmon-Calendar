<h2 id="introduction">Introduction</h2>

<p>Oahspe set out the principles of the Kosmon calendar in the Book of Inspiration. Because there were differing views about these principles, it became necessary to arrive at a resolution so that people of the world could be in accord with heaven when observing the holy days and Sabbaths.</p>

<p>These principles are centred on the orbit of the sun as the measure of the year, as opposed to counting the earth's rotations from an arbitrary historical event, or from a moon phase. Oahspe marks the beginning of this yearly orbit by the <em>northern line of the sun</em> — the Tropic of Cancer — the latitude at which the sun stands directly overhead at its northernmost point. The moment the sun reaches this line each year is the June solstice. It is this astronomical event, not any humanly devised calendar convention, that anchors the Kosmon year.</p>

<p>When naming the days of the year, Oahspe counts from the day of the solstice: Day 1 is the first day of the year, Day 7 is the first Sabbath, and so on. If Day 1 begins at the solstice moment, a question naturally follows: where on the globe does the new day — and therefore the new year — first begin?</p>

<p>In the civil calendar, the date changes at midnight at the International Date Line, a fixed boundary near the 180th meridian. But if the Kosmon year is to begin precisely at the solstice, and a day begins at midnight, then the natural starting boundary for Day 1 is the longitude where midnight coincides with the solstice moment — what this calendar calls the <strong>solstice dateline</strong> or <strong>midnight longitude point</strong>. Because the solstice moment advances roughly a quarter of a day each year through the leap-year cycle, this dateline shifts approximately 90° westward each year, cycling through all longitudes over four years. It will only rarely align near the International Date Line.</p>

<p>A consequence of anchoring the day count to this midnight longitude is that the Kosmon New Year's Day — and every Sabbath thereafter — falls on the same calendar day for all people around the world, regardless of their location. This is expressed in Oahspe's promise that <em>"the Sabbath days all around the world shall be the same day to all people, even with the travel of the sun."</em> The same principle means that holy days observed by any community using a solstice dateline can be shared across all nations on one and the same day — a matter of particular significance for communities that observe their days of worship according to a precisely designated day of the week.</p>

<p>At present, only those living near the International Date Line experience every calendar day simultaneously with the rest of the world. With a solstice dateline, that same alignment is extended to New Year's Day and all Sabbaths for all peoples, everywhere.</p>

<h3 id="global-benefit">A Wider Benefit: Global Media, Commerce and World Affairs</h3>

<p>The advantages of a universally shared calendar day are not limited to those with a religious interest. Any system in which a given day is genuinely the same day everywhere on Earth offers immediate practical benefits to a wide range of global activities.</p>

<p>For international news media and broadcasting organisations, a shared day simplifies the dating of events considerably. At present, a major event occurring late on one side of the International Date Line falls on a different calendar date to observers on the other side — creating ambiguity in headlines, archives, and broadcasts. A solstice-anchored dateline, producing a day shared simultaneously across all time zones, would eliminate this class of confusion entirely. A news event, a market movement, a summit declaration — all would carry a single unambiguous date, understood identically in Tokyo, London, New York, and Sydney.</p>

<p>For world trade and financial markets, the benefits are similarly concrete. International contracts, settlement dates, delivery schedules, and regulatory filings all depend on an agreed calendar date. Where that date is currently subject to the quirk of which side of the Date Line a transaction falls on, a universally shared day removes the ambiguity. Globally coordinated market hours, cross-border legal instruments, and multinational corporate reporting would all gain clarity.</p>

<p>Broader still, the coordination of international scientific programmes, satellite operations, global health monitoring, environmental reporting, and intergovernmental agreements all involve the precise dating of events across jurisdictions. Any framework that places all nations unambiguously on the same calendar page — without the current patchwork of date-line exceptions — offers a small but genuine improvement in the coherence of global coordination.</p>

<p>None of these benefits require the world to abandon the Gregorian calendar for everyday civil use. They arise simply from the principle — already demonstrated by the International Date Line in its current form — that a dateline anchored to a natural astronomical event can serve as a shared reference point for all peoples. The Kosmon calendar proposes that such a reference point be the June solstice: the most ancient, universal, and astronomically precise marker available.</p>

<p>This transformation is not envisioned as a decree imposed by any world authority, but as a natural and gradual development as people come to see the sense in it for their religious purpose. Only if the world of commerce were to follow — in its own time — could such a change become universal. This is distinct from the model of simultaneous global celebrations — such as world peace meditations — which require participants in many time zones to observe at whatever hour the fixed universal moment happens to fall locally, often in the middle of the night. The Kosmon approach is different: because the holy day itself is shared by all peoples on the same calendar day, each community is free to begin their celebration at a set local time of their choosing — 8 PM, or a propitious moment such as dawn, High Noon, or sunset — on the day established by the solstice dateline. That day begins for each location at its own first local midnight after the dateline midnight has passed, and the celebration thus progresses around the world with the turning of the Earth — each place taking up the holy day in turn as midnight arrives. This gives living meaning to the words of Oahspe: "And it shall come to pass that the Sabbath days all around the world shall be the same day unto all people, even with the travel of the sun."</p>

<h2 id="year">Detailed Explanation of the Sacred Kosmon Calendar</h2>

<h3>The Year</h3>

<p>A year is the orbital period of the Earth around the Sun — the time it takes the Sun to appear to complete one full circuit through the sky and return to the same position relative to the stars and the seasons. The reference point for the Kosmon calendar is the beginning of the seventh era of Kosmon.</p>

<p>The precise length of the tropical year — the time from one June solstice to the next — is approximately 365 days, 5 hours, 48 minutes, and 45 seconds. This is not a whole number of days, and no calendar that tracks the solar cycle can avoid this inconvenience. The Gregorian calendar manages it by adding a leap day every four years (with century-year corrections), so that the average year length of 365.2425 days stays close to the true tropical year of 365.24219 days — an accuracy of about one day per 3,300 years.</p>

<p>The Kosmon calendar takes a different approach. Rather than inserting leap days to reconcile the fractional remainder, it counts 365 whole days from solstice to solstice as the practical length of the year. The remaining fraction — approximately 5 hours and 49 minutes — is not incorporated into the day count. Instead, the solstice dateline simply shifts to the new midnight longitude each year, automatically reflecting the true moment of the solstice. This means the Kosmon year always begins at the actual astronomical event, not at a conventional approximation of it.</p>

<p>Monthly divisions follow the lunar cycle, each month beginning at the new moon. Because the lunar month averages approximately 29.5 days and the solar year contains approximately 12.37 lunar months, a Kosmon year will have either 12 or 13 months. The monthly cycle is allowed to overlap the new year's day and recommences at the first new moon of the new year. This approach, combining a solar year with lunar months without forcing them into arithmetic conformity, produces a calendar that tracks both the seasons and the moon with natural accuracy — and provides a reliable framework for the tables of prophecy described in Oahspe.</p>

<h4>A Brief History of Calendar Reform</h4>

<p>Prior to Julius Caesar's reform of 46 BC, the Roman calendar had drifted far from alignment with the seasons through a combination of political manipulation and accumulated error. The Julian calendar, introduced in 45 BC, established the 365-day year with a leap day every four years — a significant improvement. However, the Julian year of 365.25 days is slightly longer than the true tropical year, and over the centuries this small error accumulated. By the sixteenth century, the accumulated drift amounted to about ten days, causing the spring equinox — which the Christian church needed to fall near 21 March for the calculation of Easter — to occur instead around 11 March.</p>

<p>The Gregorian calendar, introduced in 1582, corrected this by dropping ten days from October of that year and refining the leap year rule: century years are not leap years unless divisible by 400. This gives an average year length of 365.2425 days, much closer to the tropical year. The last day of the Julian calendar was Thursday, 4 October 1582, followed immediately by Friday, 15 October 1582 in the new system. Britain and its colonies adopted the Gregorian calendar in 1752; by 1929 it had been adopted by all major nations.</p>

<p>These reforms were driven by a need to keep Easter correctly positioned relative to the spring equinox — a concern of the Church, not of astronomical precision for its own sake. The Kosmon calendar is not subject to this constraint. Its year begins at the June solstice, an astronomically fixed moment determined by observation each year, making drift impossible by design.</p>

<h4>The Tropical Year and Precession</h4>

<p>The tropical year — the year measured from solstice to solstice — has a current mean value of approximately 365.2421897 days (365 days, 5 hours, 48 minutes, 45.2 seconds). This value changes very slowly over long periods of time due to gravitational interactions with other planets.</p>

<p>It is worth noting that the Gregorian calendar was designed to track the vernal equinox year specifically, and does so with high accuracy. However, the equinox itself precesses — it moves slowly backwards through the constellations of the zodiac at a rate of about 50 arc seconds per year, completing a full circuit in approximately 25,800 years. This precession is a feature of the Earth's axial wobble, and it does not affect the accuracy of either the Gregorian or the Kosmon calendar as timekeepers of the seasons. Both remain aligned with the actual astronomical events they are designed to track.</p>

<h3 id="solar-time">Solar Time and the Equation of Time</h3>

<p>Solar time is time measured by the position of the sun in the sky. <em>Apparent solar time</em> is what a sundial reads — the actual position of the sun. <em>Mean solar time</em> is the uniform average, as shown by a well-regulated clock. The difference between the two is called the <strong>equation of time</strong>, and it arises from two causes.</p>

<p>First, the Earth's orbit is an ellipse. The Earth moves faster when closer to the sun (perihelion, around 3 January) and slower when farther away (aphelion, around 4 July). This means the sun appears to move across the sky faster at some times of year than others, contributing a variation with an amplitude of about 7.7 minutes and a period of one year.</p>

<p>Second, the Earth's axis is tilted 23.5° relative to its orbital plane. Because the sun moves along the ecliptic — a path tilted relative to the celestial equator — its daily eastward motion does not translate uniformly into a change of right ascension (the coordinate along which clock time is measured). Near the equinoxes, more of the sun's motion is in declination (north-south) and less in right ascension, effectively slowing the sun's apparent clock-time motion. Near the solstices the reverse is true. This obliquity effect contributes a variation with an amplitude of about 9.9 minutes and a period of half a year.</p>

<p>Together these two effects produce the equation of time — an irregular curve ranging from about +16 minutes 33 seconds (around 3 November, when the sundial runs fast) to about −14 minutes 6 seconds (around 12 February, when the sundial runs slow).</p>

The solstice moment is defined in terms of the sun's astronomical position (the moment it reaches its maximum declination), which is computed in Terrestrial Time and can be converted to Universal Time (UT) for practical use. The equation of time does not affect this calculation, but it does explain why local noon and clock noon differ, and why the midnight longitude point must be calculated from UT rather than from a sundial reading.</p>

<h3>Sidereal Time</h3>

<p>A <em>sidereal day</em> is the time it takes the Earth to complete one rotation relative to the distant stars: approximately 23 hours, 56 minutes, and 4.091 seconds. A solar day is approximately 4 minutes longer because during the time it takes the Earth to rotate once, it has also moved a short distance along its orbit, so the sun appears to have shifted slightly eastward and the Earth must rotate a little further to bring the sun back to its highest point.</p>

<p>Over the course of a year, this difference accumulates to one full day: there are 366.25 sidereal days in a solar year but only 365.25 solar days. Sidereal time is used by astronomers to locate stars and to define the orientation of the Earth's rotation axis, but for the purposes of the Kosmon calendar, mean solar time — and Universal Time derived from it — is the relevant standard.</p>

<h3>Standard Time and Universal Time</h3>

<p>Before the introduction of standard time zones in the late nineteenth century, every town set its clocks by local solar noon. This became impractical with the coming of railways and the telegraph, which required coordinated schedules across large distances. Sandford Fleming proposed a system of 24 standard time zones in 1879, each spanning 15° of longitude and differing from its neighbours by exactly one hour. The 1884 International Meridian Conference adopted the Greenwich meridian as the prime meridian and Greenwich Mean Time (GMT) as the world's time reference, because by that date two-thirds of all nautical charts already used it.</p>

<p>In 1928, Universal Time (UT) was adopted as a more precise replacement for GMT, defined by the rotation of the Earth and kept in step with atomic clocks through the occasional addition of leap seconds (Coordinated Universal Time, UTC). For practical purposes, UT and UTC differ by less than one second, and both serve as the reference for the Kosmon solstice calculations given in this document.</p>

<p>The key point for the Kosmon calendar is this: the solstice moment is an astronomical event that occurs at a specific instant in Universal Time. The longitude where midnight (00:00 local mean time) coincides with that instant becomes the Kosmon dateline for that year — the boundary from which Day 1 of the new year is reckoned.</p>

<h2 id="dateline">The Dateline and Universal Time</h2>

<h3>Meridians and the International Date Line</h3>

<p>A meridian is an imaginary line running from the North Pole to the South Pole along a fixed longitude. The Prime Meridian is defined as 0° and passes through the Royal Observatory at Greenwich, London — a choice made at the 1884 International Meridian Conference for reasons of navigational convention, not astronomical significance. The International Date Line runs approximately along the 180th meridian, on the opposite side of the globe from Greenwich, with diversions to avoid dividing island groups and territories.</p>

<p>Together, the Prime Meridian and the International Date Line divide the Earth into Eastern and Western Hemispheres. The date changes from one day to the next as the midnight line sweeps westward around the globe. At any given moment, the International Date Line marks the boundary between the two calendar dates currently in effect on Earth.</p>

<p>Both the Prime Meridian and the International Date Line are arbitrary human conventions. No international treaty fixes the exact position of the Date Line; all nations define their own time zones. It is placed in the Pacific Ocean for practical reasons — to minimise the inconvenience of a date change crossing inhabited land. As the example of Kiribati illustrates, when a dateline does bisect a nation's territory, it creates real difficulties: upon independence in 1979, the new republic found government offices on opposite sides of the line able to communicate only on the four weekdays when both sides simultaneously experienced working days. Kiribati resolved this in 1995 by moving the date line eastward to encompass all of its territory.</p>

<h3>Both the Date Line and Universal Time Are Arbitrary</h3>

<p>The question of which calendar day the Kosmon New Year falls in — for any given country — was raised in an early discussion by an Oahspe community member:</p>

<blockquote>"Regarding split days, where the solstice moment falls after midnight one part of the world, and before midnight on another, this indicates the need for an agreed upon Universal Time reference point which I believe is somewhat arbitrary."</blockquote>

<p>The observation is correct: the choice of dateline is arbitrary. The ceaseless 24-hour cycle of midnight, dawn, noon, and sunset repeats identically at every location on Earth. The date changes at midnight wherever you stand — the dateline simply marks the agreed boundary at which the count of the calendar date officially resets. Moving that boundary to the solstice midnight longitude does not alter the experience of any individual day; it only relocates the point on the globe where the new year's count begins.</p>

<p>Confusion about which day an event falls in — when comparing locations in different countries — arises not from the dateline itself, but from the difference in local time between one time zone and another. This comparison works identically whether or not the dateline is at Greenwich's antipode or at the solstice midnight longitude.</p>

<p>The Prime Meridian is likewise an arbitrary convention, set at Greenwich by historical precedent. For the Kosmon calendar, a floating dateline — reset each year to the solstice midnight longitude — serves the religious purpose of ensuring that New Year's Day and all Sabbaths are celebrated on the same calendar day around the world. Since the solstice midnight longitude advances approximately 90° westward each year through the four-year cycle (as the fractional-day remainder accumulates), it will pass through different continents in different years, but this is of no concern to those observing the holy days. The day count always starts afresh from the true solstice moment.</p>

<p>A further observation, from another community member:</p>
<blockquote>"The only thing a time zone does is to more or less average out when exactly the midnight hour is for that geographical area within the standard time zone."</blockquote>

<p>This is correct. Standard time zones are averages; only at the central meridian of a zone does clock midnight correspond exactly to solar midnight. The Kosmon dateline, by contrast, is defined by true solar midnight at the solstice moment — the most astronomically precise reference available.</p>

<p>The dateline is reset each year to the longitude where midnight falls at the moment of the solstice. The position of existing civil time zones is not affected. What changes is only this: the day on which the Kosmon New Year begins — and by extension all Sabbaths of that year — is now the same calendar day for all peoples around the world.</p>

<p>Because of the requirement to have all peoples celebrate the holy days on the same day, and for the weekly Sabbath to fall on the same day at any location around the globe, we take the moment when the sun reaches the Northern Line (the Tropic of Cancer) as our universal reference. The fact that this moment varies slightly from year to year makes no practical difference in ordinary life and can be disregarded by most people most of the time.</p>

<h2 id="solstice-calc">Calculating the Solstice Dateline</h2>

<p>The moon phase times given in the Kosmon Calendar tables below are in <strong>UKT (Universal Kosmon Time)</strong>. Moon phase times are normally published in Greenwich time (UT), but to position them correctly within the Kosmon calendar, they must be converted from UT to UKT. This is done by subtracting the time difference between the new dateline longitude and Greenwich.</p>

<p>The conversion formula is straightforward: the solstice occurs at a known UT time. The solstice dateline is the meridian where local midnight coincides with that UT time. If the solstice occurs at time <em>T</em> (UT), then the dateline longitude is:</p>

<p class="formula">Dateline longitude = (T in hours × 15°) West of Greenwich</p>

<p>For example, if the solstice occurs at 06:46 UT, the dateline is 06.767 hours × 15° = <strong>101.5° West of Greenwich</strong> — midnight near Denver, Colorado.</p>

<p>To convert a moon phase time from UT to UKT, subtract the solstice time (in hours) from the phase time.</p>

<p><strong>Example (2005):</strong><br>
Solstice: 21 June 2005, 06:46 UT<br>
New Moon (First Month): 6 July 2005, 12:02 UT<br>
UKT conversion: 12:02 − 06:46 = <strong>05:16 UKT</strong></p>

<p>Moon phase times are obtained from the U.S. Naval Observatory (USNO) website. Times in brackets in the calendar tables below represent UKT; unbracketed times are UT (Greenwich time).</p>

<p>In the calendar tables, the solstice moment, dateline longitude, and nearest major location are noted at the transition between Old Year's Day and New Year's Day.</p>

<p>At the solstice, the line of intersection between the solar plane and the celestial equatorial plane — the line connecting the two orbital nodes — stands perpendicular to the direction of the sun. This is the precise astronomical moment when the sun reaches the Northern Line (the Tropic of Cancer). Among all the longitudes of the Earth that face the sun in succession as the Earth rotates, there is one longitude that faces the sun exactly at local High Noon at this moment. Along this meridian, the sun's axis is parallel to the Earth's axis, and all locations on this meridian face the sun squarely at noon.</p>

<p>At this exact moment, the four principal stations of the celestial sphere — the two solstices and the two equinoxes, known as the <a href="http://pperov.angelfire.com/galactic.html#colures">colures</a> — stand in the four cardinal directions of the sky. The alignment is further signalled by the vertical culmination on the meridian of the pole of the ecliptic together with the north celestial pole. This first meridian to come into alignment at the solstice moment marks the beginning of the first day of the Faithist Sacred Year, whose hours begin at the midnight immediately preceding this High Noon.</p>

<p>Therefore, the universal Old Year's Day begins at High Noon along the solstice meridian. For every other location around the world, the holy day begins at its own local midnight — the first midnight to follow the dateline midnight — as the turning of the Earth carries each place in turn through its day's beginning. This is how <em>"it shall come to pass that the Sabbath days all around the world shall be the same day unto all people, even with the travel of the sun."</em></p>

<h2 id="early">Early Discussion</h2>

<p>Steven's response to an early query (some years ago):</p>

<blockquote>This is an old sore to some Oahspeans and has divided them to a certain extent in the past. The 1882 edition says it's the summer solstice; the 1891 version says it's the winter solstice; the English version says that Old Year's Day is in the winter. It depends on which book you prefer, or which book you read first, or where you want Old/New Year's Day to be. Which book is correct? The original? Or the ones that came at a later time? <h3>An Excerpt of Joan Greer's Clarification</h3>

<p>Much has been written in the past 30 or so years over the editing which was done to the 1882 Edition of Oahspe. The 1891 Edition is the result of that editing. The Wing Anderson Edition and the English Edition are in essence the 1891 Edition. The editing of particular interest in the discussion of the New Year is found in Book of Inspiration, Chapter XIV. In the 1882 Edition the verses in question read as follows:</p>

<blockquote>
  <em>2. The northern line of the sun shall be the end of the year, and it shall be called the last day of the old year, saith Jehovih.<br>
  3. And the first day thereafter, when the sun starteth on his southern course, shall be the beginning of the year, and it shall be called the new year's day.</em>
  <cite>(Quoted from an actual 1882 Edition)</cite>
</blockquote>

<p>This was changed in the 1891 edition to read as follows:</p>

<blockquote>
  <em>2. The shortest day on the northern line of the sun shall be the end of the year, and shall be called old year's day, saith Jehovih.<br>
  3. And the first day thereafter, when the sun on his southern course starteth towards the north, shall be the beginning of the year, and shall be called new year's day.</em>
  <cite>(Quoted from the English Edition)</cite>
</blockquote>

<p>When you study these two passages, it becomes clear that the meaning of "the northern line" holds the key. In the 1882 Edition, the northern line is a <em>place</em> the sun reaches — a latitude. In the 1891 edition, "the northern line" is treated as a <em>direction</em> of travel. The 1882 version says the sun starts its southern course <em>from</em> the northern line; the 1891 version says the sun travels <em>toward</em> the north, implying the year begins at the winter solstice rather than the summer.</p>

<p>The following passage is consistent across all editions of Oahspe, and it defines the Northern Line of the sun. Book of Fragapatti, Son of Jehovih, Chapter XIX:</p>

<blockquote>
  <em>1. A thousand miles north of <strong>the northern line of the sun on the earth</strong>, in the middle betwixt the east and west front of North Guatama, and from the earth upward, and without intervening space, five hundred miles, had Yaton'te founded his kingdom.</em>
</blockquote>

<img src="Yatonte.jpg" alt="Map showing Yatonte's kingdom location">
<p class="img-caption">Yatonte's kingdom location marked by the pink circle, near Chickasha (close to Oklahoma City). The purple line represents 1,000 miles from the Tropic of Cancer. The blue and green lines, of equal length, mark the east and west edges of the North Guataman continent.</p>

<p>This passage places Yaton'te's kingdom 1,000 miles north of the northern line of the sun on the earth — which, measuring 1,000 miles north of the Tropic of Cancer (approximately 23.5°N), places it near 33°N latitude, consistent with the Oklahoma City region. This confirms that "the northern line of the sun on the earth" is the Tropic of Cancer — a fixed geographical line, not a direction of travel. The 1882 Edition is therefore the correct one: the northern line is the place the sun reaches at the Summer Solstice, and it is this moment that marks the Old Year/New Year transition.</p>

<h2 id="solstice">Solstice and the Northern Line</h2>

<p>At the summer solstice the sun reaches its northernmost point in the sky. In the northern hemisphere this is the longest day; in the southern hemisphere it is the shortest. This event is simultaneous for both hemispheres — it occurs at the same moment everywhere on Earth. The northern line, being a geographic latitude (the Tropic of Cancer), is equally a global marker regardless of which hemisphere the observer is in. Oahspe's use of it as the year's boundary is therefore not ambiguous or hemisphere-specific: the sun reaches the northern line on the same day for all peoples of the world.</p>

<p>The Earth's axis is tilted approximately 23.5° from a right angle to its orbital plane. At the summer solstice, this tilt inclines the northern hemisphere most directly toward the sun. At the winter solstice, the axis is inclined most directly away. The word "solstice" derives from the Latin <em>sol</em> (sun) and <em>sistere</em> (to stand still) — because for a day or so around each solstice the sun's rising and setting positions on the horizon appear almost stationary, having reached their extreme northerly or southerly extent. At the equinoxes, by contrast, the sunrise and sunset points shift most rapidly along the horizon.</p>

<p>The Tropic of Cancer (approximately 23.5°N) and the Tropic of Capricorn (approximately 23.5°S) mark the extreme latitudes at which the sun stands directly overhead — at noon on the summer and winter solstices respectively. These tropics were defined by the Greek geographer Eratosthenes around 230 BC as the boundaries of the inhabited zones of the Earth. The sun's rays fall vertically on the equator at the equinoxes, and vertically on the tropics at the solstices. Everywhere outside the tropics, the sun never stands directly overhead.</p>

<p>As the dictionary confirms, the Tropic of Cancer is "the northernmost latitude reached by the overhead sun." This is precisely what Oahspe calls the northern line of the sun on the earth. The identification is unambiguous.</p>

<h3>Observing the Solstice Moment</h3>

<p>The day of the equinox can be determined by finding the midpoint between the summer and winter solstice sunrise positions on the horizon, or by tracking the noon shadow of an upright obelisk until it stops shortening and begins to lengthen again. The midnight of the summer solstice can be determined using a <a href="http://pperov.angelfire.com/galactic.html#gau">gau</a> — an instrument with an attached plumb line. The solstice midnight is the moment when the pole of the ecliptic aligns on the meridian with the north celestial pole (Polaris). In practice, it is easier to align the gau with a line between Polaris and Betelgeuse (in Orion), since the pole of the ecliptic — located in the constellation Draco — has no bright star to mark it directly.</p>

<h2 id="oahspe-text">Oahspe Textual Clarification: The Meaning of "Hadan"</h2>

<p>The 1882 Oahspe records the following (from Book of Inspiration):</p>

<blockquote>
  <em>God said to Ha'jah: Because thou hast prospered my kingdom during one whole year, thou shalt be my companion and assistant, with power and wisdom to superintend all matters not direct with my Lords.<br>
  Behold, this day have I set apart as a new day in heaven and earth; because on this day the sun taketh its course <strong>from the north line</strong>, and from this time forth it shall be called the new year's day. So shall it be, from this time forth, the day of the relief watch in Hored.</em>
</blockquote>

<p>The English (1891) Oahspe renders the same passage as:</p>

<blockquote>
  <em>Behold, this day have I set apart as a new day in heaven and earth; because on this day the sun taketh its course <strong>for the Hadan [north] line</strong> and from this time forth it shall be called the new year's day.</em>
</blockquote>

<p>The 1891 English Edition translates "Hadan" as meaning "north," and reads the phrase as "taking its course for the north line" — implying the sun is heading northward, which would place the New Year at the winter solstice. However, the 1891 glossary defines "Hadan" as <em>atmospherea</em>, not as "north." This usage of "Hadan" to mean north appears only once in the English Oahspe, and nowhere else. It is therefore a doubtful translation.</p>

<p>The Oahspe text in the Book of Wars Against Jehovih Ch. XLVIII:26 records:</p>

<blockquote>
  <em>"the observing line shall be with the apex of the Hidan vortex, which lieth in the median line of the variation of the north star."</em>
</blockquote>

<p>Robert Bauval's work on the orientation of ancient monuments provides a useful parallel. He observes:</p>

<blockquote>"When an equinox is rising due-east on the horizon, the prime meridian or 'great circle' will loop directly above, passing through the north celestial pole and also through the pole of the ecliptic. The pole of the ecliptic is always on the prime meridian line when an equinox point is rising in the east. The pole of the ecliptic will be at upper culmination at the meridian (about 53.5° altitude) only when the vernal point (spring equinox) is rising due-east. When this happens, all the main 'stations' (or 'colures') of the sky (the two equinoxes and solstices) will be found in the four cardinal directions of the celestial landscape."</blockquote>

<p>The "apex of the Hidan vortex" is therefore most naturally identified with the north pole of the ecliptic — an indicator of the sun's own axis of reference — rather than the North Star. "Hidan Sun" and "Hidan Vortex" refer to our own sun and its vortex, and the "median line of the variation of the north star" is the meridian — the line about which the North Star appears to trace its small daily circle due to the Earth's rotation.</p>

<p>With this understanding, the phrase "on this day the sun taketh its course for the Hadan [north] line" is better read as: on this day the sun's course reaches its apex relative to the solar vortex axis — that is, the sun reaches the Tropic of Cancer, the northern limit of its annual arc. This interpretation is consistent with the 1882 text ("from the north line"), with the fixed geographical meaning of "the northern line of the sun on the earth" as the Tropic of Cancer, and with the placement of Yaton'te's kingdom as described in the Book of Fragapatti.</p>

<p>The 1882 Oahspe is therefore the authoritative text on this point. The New Year begins at the June solstice, when the sun departs from its northernmost latitude and begins its return southward.</p>
